# 09 — Brand Architecture & Essence: From "Two Brothers Who DJ" to a Mediterranean Music & Wellness House

## 1. TL;DR — explicit recommendations

The dashboard reads as too monetization-focused because the underlying brand architecture has never been declared. Without a clear architecture, every gig — Madefor, Mlab, Sync, a sponsor activation, a personal club night — competes for the same "Lowtide" label and the same Instagram grid, which forces the dashboard to default to the only legible axis left: revenue. Fix the architecture and the dashboard's tone changes by itself.

**Q1 — Brand architecture.** Recommendation: **Option C, the Service-Agency model, with a clear master brand ("Lowtide") and four named lanes — Afterglow, Listening, Privé, Flagship.** Lowtide is the agency / curatorial house. The four lanes are products, each with its own posture, audience, price band, and sponsor lane. This is the only architecture that lets Alberto and his brother grow as DJs, take Mlab-aligned underground nights without poisoning Devialet, run Madefor-style mainstream-runner nights without dragging the curated lane down, and book Devialet-tier flagships without those flagships having to carry the whole brand. The 90-day test: ship Afterglow #1 + Listening #1 under the new architecture, see if Mlab will sign the brand-wall memo for Privé, and measure whether the press/sponsor lanes stay separated. If Mlab refuses the brand wall, fall back to a House-of-Brands posture and let Privé walk under Mlab's roof.

**Q2 — Individual artist aliases.** Recommendation: **Yes, but in a phased way.** Each brother takes a private artist alias for their solo SoundCloud/Spotify and for selection-driven Mlab/Privé bookings. The duo billing "Lowtide" stays the public face for now. In 12 months, when each alias has 5+ tracks and a defensible solo identity, they reintroduce the aliases publicly so Lowtide becomes "Alias 1 + Alias 2, also known as Lowtide" — the Bedouin / Adam Ten / Adriatique architecture. This protects underground credibility, opens parallel growth paths, and gives optionality without doubling the IG/content load on day one (the aliases live on SoundCloud/RA/Beatport first, IG second).

**Q3 — Service-agency framing.** Recommendation: **Lowtide is structured like a small Brunch Electronik or Innervisions — a music-house with a portfolio of named formats**, not a single-format brand. Each format has its own visual, its own pricing logic, its own sponsor lane, and its own internal owner. The dashboard reframes around this: not a P&L grid with gigs flowing through one funnel, but a 2×4 matrix of *lane × pillar* that shows whether each lane is feeding the four essence pillars (improving as DJs, community, Devialet-tier curation, not-losing-essence-for-money). That matrix is the dashboard's new spine.

The rest of this doc walks each of the three answers in depth, then proposes the four lanes, the essence pillars, the Valencia venue prospecting, and the dashboard implications.

## 2. Brand architecture deep-dive

### 2.1 The three options, restated and stress-tested

**A) Single master brand "Lowtide Ritual" with sub-formats.** Everything ships under Lowtide. Afterglow, Listening, Privé are sub-products. Like Apple → iPhone, iPad, Mac. Pros: one brand to compound, one IG audience, one email list, one sponsor pitch. Cons: the curated/wellness halo and the underground/club halo *cannot* live under the same logo without one corrupting the other (see doc 06, §5). The first time a Devialet brand-safety reviewer scrolls @lowtideritual and sees a Privé club night two posts above a Vespertina listening session, the deck collapses. A is operationally simple and strategically fatal at the wedge Lowtide is trying to win.

**B) House-of-brands.** Lowtide stays curated/Devialet-coded. Afterglow becomes its own brand (co-owned with Madefor or another run-club). Privé becomes its own brand (co-owned with Mlab). Like P&G → Tide, Ariel, Pampers. Pros: total isolation between lanes; each brand can grow on its own logic and own sponsors. Cons: triples the surface area to operate (3 IGs, 3 brands, 3 visual systems, 3 inboxes); for a 2-person team this is a recipe for mediocrity in all three; loses the compounding benefit of one shared audience; obscures the single biggest strategic asset Alberto has, which is *that the same curators are doing all of this* — the meta-story of "two brothers across four lanes" is the brand.

**C) Service-agency model, master + lanes.** Lowtide is the master, the agency, the curatorial house. Each lane has a named product identity that travels with Lowtide as its parent. Like Innervisions → Lossless, Secret Sundaze; or Crosstown Rebels → Get Lost, Day Zero, Rebellion; or Brunch Electronik → Brunch In The Park, Brunch In The City, Brunch -On the Beach-; or Soho House music programming → DJ Sets, Soho Skin, House Notes. Pros: keeps the master compounding, lets each lane have its own posture (sober/loud, curated/underground, mainstream/niche) without poisoning the master, allows separate sponsor lanes and separate press lanes, gives Alberto and his brother room to grow as DJs across three different musical postures. Cons: requires discipline — the lanes must be visually distinct enough that a sponsor reviewer can scroll the master and parse them as separate products, but consistent enough that Lowtide's master story still reads as one house. This is the hardest architecture to execute and the one that scales best.

### 2.2 Real-world precedents

**Brunch Electronik (Barcelona, since 2013).** The team behind Brunch operates a portfolio: *Brunch In The Park* (the Sunday daytime flagship at Poble Espanyol, 4–6K cap, family-friendly, Estrella Damm-sponsored, the mainstream-safe halo); *Brunch In The City* (smaller-cap urban editions); *Brunch -On the Beach-* (summer beachfront editions); *Brunch Electronik Lisbon / Madrid* (city franchises). They never let their underground-late-night programming carry the Brunch IP — when their crew wants to play harder/later, it's under different vehicles. The Brunch master brand stays sober-coded, daytime-coded, sponsor-safe. **This is the Lowtide-as-master playbook executed cleanly.** Source: pattern observation, training data through 2026; verify against `brunchelectronik.com`. `[verify]`

**Innervisions (Berlin, since 2005, founded by Âme + Dixon).** Innervisions is the master label. *Lossless* is a sub-label for more ambient/non-dancefloor work. *Lost in a Moment* is the touring party series. *Secret Sundaze* is a separate but allied operator that often shares lineups. Each carries its own visual, its own A&R, its own audience. Âme (Kristian Beyer + Frank Wiedemann) and Dixon (Steffen Berkhahn) operate as both solo artists and label co-owners — the **alias + label pair** is the architecture, not just the parent brand. **This is the model for "two brothers who DJ build a house, not just a duo."** `[verify]` against `innervisions.shop`.

**Crosstown Rebels (London, Damian Lazarus, since 2003).** Master label. *Get Lost* is the touring party brand (Miami WMC, Ibiza, Tulum) — sponsor-friendly, cosmopolitan, daytime-into-night. *Day Zero* is the destination festival brand (Tulum jungle, Bali, Mumbai) — premium-priced, "transformational," more ritual-coded, the highest-margin product. *Rebellion* is the more underground sub-label. Damian Lazarus is the public face but each property has its own visual system, its own pricing, its own venue archetype. **This is the closest direct analog to where Lowtide is trying to go — one curator, multiple branded properties at different price points and intentions.** `[verify]` against `crosstownrebels.com`.

**Resident Advisor (RA).** Master editorial brand. *RA Live* is the broadcast/streaming product. *RA Sessions* is the DJ-mix product. *RA Exchange* is the podcast. *RA Pro* is the B2B/professional layer. RA never lets the streaming product cannibalise the editorial brand or vice versa. **The lesson: a curatorial house can run media + events + B2B in parallel, but only if each has its own product name and its own posture under the master.**

**Soho House music programming.** Soho House operates an in-house music team that programs across the houses (DJ residencies, in-house broadcasts, the Soho House Sounds Spotify, periodic listening events). The team works with three coded postures: (a) sundowner / aperitivo, (b) members' party / late-night, (c) listening / hi-fi. Each posture has different curators, different volume rules, different alcohol postures, different photographic style. **The lesson: the same operator can run three musical postures simultaneously if each has explicit rules that travel with the booking.**

**Counter-example — Daybreaker.** Daybreaker stayed disciplined: it never let itself become a nighttime brand, even when chapter captains pushed for it. Result: 12+ years in, still sponsor-safe, still wellness-coded, still bookable by Lululemon and Equinox. The lesson: the master brand has *rules of refusal*. Lowtide's master must too. The rules of refusal are the point — they're what makes the lanes possible.

### 2.3 Why C wins for Lowtide specifically

Three reasons.

1. **The meta-story IS the brand.** "Two brothers from Valencia who curate music across mainstream-running mornings, curated-listening dusks, and underground nights" is more interesting than any single one of those lanes alone. House-of-Brands (B) destroys this story. Single-brand (A) flattens it. Only the agency / lanes model (C) lets the meta-story be the public-facing arc while each lane lives on its own internal logic.
2. **Sponsor lanes diverge.** Hoka and Devialet and Estrella Damm and a Mlab-credible underground promoter all want different things from Lowtide's surface. C is the only architecture where each can find what they want without seeing what they don't.
3. **Selection growth.** Alberto and his brother are *improving as DJs.* They need three different selection muscles — mainstream-tolerant for runners, deep-curated for Listening, underground-credible for Privé. Forcing one brand to carry all three muscles slows the growth on each. Four lanes give each muscle its own gym.

### 2.4 90-day test

**Goal:** validate that the four-lane architecture works in practice before committing visual systems, microsites, or any irreversible naming.

**Days 0–14.**
- Write a one-page "architecture memo." Master brand = Lowtide. Four lanes named (Afterglow, Listening, Privé, Flagship — see §5). For each lane: positioning, audience, price band, music posture, refusals, sponsor lane, internal owner. This memo lives in `03-experiencias/lowtide/PROJECT.md` as the source of truth. Every gig from this point forward is tagged with one lane.
- Sit down with Mlab's named decision-maker (per doc 06, §7) and present the architecture. Ask: is Mlab OK that Privé is a *Lowtide-house* lane (with a Mlab co-credit), or does Mlab want Privé to be a Mlab-house lane (with a Lowtide guest credit)? Either is acceptable — one brand wall is what matters. If Mlab refuses any wall, this is the fork: collapse to A and Privé becomes a guest collaboration outside the architecture (Alberto plays as a guest at Mlab nights, no co-branding).

**Days 15–45.**
- Ship **Afterglow #1** — co-branded with Madefor, post-race recovery format, 60–80 cap, mainstream-tolerant selection (this is where runners-don't-dance-much-but-want-mainstream gets served, by design). Document everything as a Lowtide-Afterglow visual / press unit. Single named photographer, no club-coded imagery.
- Ship **Listening #1** — Vespertina-format from doc 08, 80–100 cap, dusk listening circle, Devialet-tier production. Single editorial photographer, sober-coded, no run-club imagery either. The point of doing them within 30 days of each other is to *prove* visually that the same master brand can carry both lanes without confusion.

**Days 46–90.**
- Ship **Privé #1** under the brand wall agreed with Mlab. Different photographer, different visual register, *not posted on the Lowtide grid.* Alberto and brother appear under their artist aliases (see §3) on the lineup, not as "Lowtide."
- **Decision gate at day 90.** Hold C if and only if: (a) Afterglow #1 generated 1 sponsor conversation, (b) Listening #1 generated 1 editorial press placement and a Devialet conversation, (c) Privé #1 was profitable on door without contaminating the Lowtide grid (zero alcohol-led photos on @lowtide), (d) Alberto and brother feel each event made them better DJs in a different way. If any of these fail, demote that lane (kill or merge) and reduce to three lanes.

**The single non-negotiable test:** at day 90, can a Devialet brand-safety reviewer scroll the @lowtide grid and see only Listening + Flagship content, with Afterglow as a clearly-labeled sister product, and zero Privé content? If yes, C works. If no, C has been executed sloppily and needs another month of discipline before it scales.

## 3. Individual artist aliases

### 3.1 Recommendation — yes, in two phases

**Phase 1 (months 0–12): private aliases.** Each brother picks a private artist alias for SoundCloud, Spotify, Beatport, RA, Beatport, and underground bookings. The aliases play Mlab/Privé nights and sit on the underground side of the calendar. Lowtide as a duo billing remains the public face for B2B, Listening, Afterglow, Flagship. The aliases are *known to those who care* (the underground crowd discovers them through Privé and Mlab) and *invisible to those who don't* (the Hoka brand manager sees only Lowtide).

**Phase 2 (months 12–24): public aliases under the duo.** Once each alias has 4–6 released tracks (originals or edits), 1+ guest mix on a respectable show (Slowciety, Test Pressing, Beats In Space, Rinse, Dekmantel mix series), and a defensible solo identity, the duo billing becomes "[Alias 1] + [Alias 2], also known as Lowtide." This is the Bedouin / Adam Ten / Adriatique architecture: the duo is the brand, the aliases are the artists, the label/agency (Lowtide) is the house above both.

### 3.2 Why this matters more than it looks

Three structural reasons.

1. **Underground credibility is single-track.** A duo named after a vibe ("Lowtide") will never be booked at a real underground night by a serious promoter — that promoter wants an artist name with a discography. Without aliases, Mlab and any future Privé-tier collaborators are doing Alberto a favor every time they book "Lowtide." With aliases, the booking becomes neutral: they book the alias because the discography earns it, and Lowtide's underground access stops depending on social capital.
2. **Spotify/SoundCloud growth paths diverge.** A duo account on Spotify is a single growth curve; two aliases plus a duo account is three. The maths matters because B2B sponsors increasingly look at monthly listeners as a credibility number, and three curves give Lowtide three different stories to tell different sponsors.
3. **Optionality.** If one brother takes a year off to travel, focus on production, or do a long-form retreat, the alias survives without dragging the duo to zero. The duo can also ship duo releases under "Lowtide" without confusing anyone — that's a third release lane.

### 3.3 Naming-strategy guidance

**Rules of thumb that work for this category.**

- **Pronounceable in Spanish and English.** Both brothers are operating between Valencia and the international wellness traveler. Don't pick a name that a Hoka brand manager has to ask twice how to say.
- **Not a real word that anchors a meaning.** "Marea" or "Salar" or "Dusk" all sound great but they collide with Lowtide's own concept-vocabulary (see doc 08) and they collapse the alias into the master. Pick something with no hard meaning — a coined word, a compound, a place name from outside Spain so it doesn't read as derivative.
- **Two syllables, max three.** DJ aliases that work — Bedouin, Innellea, Mind Against, Adriatique, Adam Ten, Pional, Talabot — all hit a 2–3 syllable ceiling. Easier to chant, easier to print, easier to remember.
- **Don't start with "DJ."** Outdated.
- **Don't use the brother's first name alone.** Reduces optionality, ties to a single biography. Alias should be a vessel, not a portrait.
- **Search-distinct.** Google the candidate name + "DJ" before committing. If there's an existing Soundcloud/RA artist with the same name in any genre, walk away. RA artist pages are forever; collisions kill discoverability.
- **Trademark-safe in EU.** Cheap check via EUIPO TMView before committing — it costs nothing and prevents a year-3 cease-and-desist from a German techno producer with the same name.

**Practical examples from the model.**
- **Bedouin** — Tamer Malki + Rami Abouanni — duo name is the brand, both members are credited but rarely take separate solo billing publicly. Saving Grace label sits underneath. `[verify]`
- **Adam Ten** — solo artist + Mita is his frequent partner; both have solo aliases (Adam Ten, Mita) and a duo billing. They run the *Maccabi House* label as the parent. `[verify]`
- **Adriatique** — Adrian Schweizer + Adrian Shala — duo billing with both members named on the lineup as "Adriatique" never as two separate aliases. They run *Siamese* as label parent. `[verify]`
- **Mind Against** — brothers Alessandro and Federico Fognini — duo billing, no solo aliases publicly though both have explored separate work. `[verify]`
- **Tale of Us** — Carmine Conte + Matteo Milleri — operated as a duo, then split (2022). Carmine kept the Tale of Us name as solo; Matteo launched *Anyma*. **The cautionary lesson:** without strong individual aliases pre-existing, a duo split is brand-destroying for one party. Aliases are the *insurance policy* against a future split.

### 3.4 The cost honestly stated

Two new aliases means two more Spotify-for-Artists accounts to maintain, two more SoundCloud profiles, and two more 1-mix-per-quarter content lines. It is real work. The IG load is the part to cap aggressively: **the aliases live on SoundCloud, Beatport, RA, and Spotify first; IG is optional in year one.** A single private IG per alias, posted to 4–6× per year (one per release, one per Privé night), is enough.

### 3.5 What to do if Alberto and his brother disagree

This decision is structural enough that it can't be split — both must use aliases or neither. If one wants aliases and the other doesn't, the right outcome is **neither uses aliases publicly for now, and the one who wants the alias releases solo work under a working name on SoundCloud only, untagged from Lowtide.** That keeps optionality alive without forcing the architecture decision.

## 4. Service-agency framing — how the operators do it

### 4.1 Brunch Electronik

Multi-brand portfolio under one operator (Trumpet Festivals SL, Barcelona). The internal architecture (`[verify]`):
- **Brunch In The Park** — Sunday daytime flagship, Poble Espanyol, ~4–6K cap. The mainstream-safe, sponsor-saturated halo product. Estrella Damm, JBL, Movistar, Ballantine's are recurring partners.
- **Brunch In The City** — smaller-cap urban indoor editions. Different visual register, dirtier and warmer.
- **Brunch -On the Beach-** — summer beachfront editions, sun-coded, Mediterranean visual.
- **Brunch Electronik Lisbon / Madrid** — city franchises with local partners. Same template, local talent.
- **Studio sessions** — a smaller, more curated content product (broadcast / live recording).

Each format has its own poster style, its own ticket band (€25–35 standard, €60+ premium tiers for B-In-The-Park), its own talent posture (Brunch In The Park gets the international headliners; Brunch In The City gets the deeper / underground-leaning bookings the headliners alone can't justify). They never let any of the smaller formats undermine the master brand's family-friendly halo. **The lane discipline is what makes the sponsor stack possible.**

### 4.2 Innervisions

Founded 2005 by Âme (Kristian Beyer + Frank Wiedemann) and Dixon (Steffen Berkhahn). The architecture:
- **Innervisions** — master label, vinyl-first, the curated A&R house.
- **Lossless** — sub-label for more abstract, ambient, non-dancefloor work. Different cover-art template, different release cadence.
- **Lost in a Moment** — the touring party series, originally tied to specific architectural locations (a vineyard, a mountain). Premium-priced, low-cap, one-off-feel.
- The members operate as solo artists (Dixon, Âme — itself a duo), with frequent solo bookings and shared bookings under the label.

The lesson for Lowtide: **the label/agency is the parent, the parties are products, the artists are people, and all three layers are visible to the audience without confusing one for another.** The name "Innervisions" carries the curatorial promise; the artist names carry the discography; the parties carry the venue and the night. Three different surfaces, one house. `[verify]` `innervisions.shop`.

### 4.3 Crosstown Rebels

Damian Lazarus, founded 2003. Architecture:
- **Crosstown Rebels** — master label, ~20 releases/year.
- **Get Lost** — the touring party brand. Has run editions at Miami Music Week, Ibiza (Pacha, DC10), Tulum, Mykonos, Burning Man week. Premium-priced, sunrise-coded, sponsor-friendly daytime-into-night posture. The mainstream-safe halo product.
- **Day Zero** — destination festival brand. Tulum (jungle, the original), Bali, Mumbai, Sri Lanka. Premium-priced (€80–250 multi-day passes), "transformational," ritual-coded, the highest-margin product. Travel-tourism-coded.
- **Rebellion** — the more underground sub-label, edgier cover art, harder selection.

Damian Lazarus is the public face but never carries any of these properties alone — each has its own art direction, its own venue archetype, its own crowd. **The closest direct analog to where Lowtide is going.** A single curator, multiple branded properties at multiple price points, each living at its own intentional altitude. `[verify]` `crosstownrebels.com`.

### 4.4 RA (Resident Advisor)

Editorial-first house with a portfolio:
- **RA Editorial** — the news + reviews + features magazine.
- **RA Live** — broadcast / streaming product (RA Live mixes, RA Sessions).
- **RA Exchange** — long-form podcast.
- **RA Tickets** — the events platform / B2B layer.
- **RA Pro** — the professional / industry layer.

RA's lesson is more relevant to the *content* side of Lowtide than the events side. **The principle: a curatorial house is allowed to extend into media, broadcast, and B2B as long as each extension has its own product name, its own posture, and doesn't dilute the master.** For Lowtide, this is permission to eventually launch a *Lowtide Mix Series* (broadcast), *The Tide Letter* (the newsletter, doc 07), *Lowtide Studio* (the B2B agency layer) — each as a distinct product line under the master.

### 4.5 Soho House music programming

Soho House operates a global in-house music team programming across 40+ houses. The architecture (`[verify]`, internal):
- **Three musical postures per house** — sundowner / aperitivo (sober-leaning, light), members' party / late-night (alcohol-friendly, social), and listening / hi-fi (the most curated, often around dinner or a private room).
- **Each posture has different curators, different volume rules, different photographic style.** A photographer briefed for the listening posture is *not* the same person who shoots the late-night party.
- **The master brand (Soho House) carries the membership promise; the postures carry the programming.** Members understand they get all three by belonging.

The lesson: **the same operator can run three or more musical postures simultaneously, profitably, and without diluting the master, *if* the rules per posture are explicit and travel with every booking.** This is the operating model Lowtide should copy at the agency level — not three brands, three postures, codified.

### 4.6 The convergent pattern

Across Brunch, Innervisions, Crosstown Rebels, RA, and Soho House music, the same architectural principles repeat:

1. **The master brand carries the curatorial promise; the lanes carry the programming.**
2. **Each lane has its own visual, its own price, its own venue archetype, its own sponsor lane.**
3. **The master never carries the underground product on its primary mark.** Privé / underground / harder programming always sits on a sub-mark or a co-owned sibling, never on the master.
4. **The curators are the human face; the lanes are the products; the agency is the institution.** Three layers, all simultaneously visible.
5. **Rules of refusal are codified.** Brunch refuses peak-time / late-night. Daybreaker refuses alcohol. Devialet refuses club PA. The refusals are the brand.

These five principles are the inheritance Lowtide should adopt verbatim.

## 5. The four lanes proposed

Each of the four lanes below is a *product* under the Lowtide master. Each has a posture, an audience, a music language, a price band, a venue archetype, an internal owner, and a sponsor lane. The lanes must not be operationally collapsed — even though Alberto (or his brother) might play at all four, the products themselves are distinct.

### 5.1 Afterglow — the post-race / mainstream-runner lane

- **Positioning.** The post-race / post-run recovery format. Co-branded with Madefor (or any run-club partner). Mainstream-tolerant selection because, per Alberto's discovery, *runners don't dance much, and when they do they want mainstream.* Honor that. The product is recovery + groove, not a dance floor.
- **Audience.** Runners 22–40, brand-funded. Free or token-priced (€5–15) because the brand pays. Average run-club regular plus a curious new-runner tail.
- **Music posture.** 90–115 BPM, sun-coded, vocal-tolerant, mainstream-leaning daytime house and disco. Think NTS' weekend rotation, Folamour's lighter side, Kerri Chandler's vocals, Honey Dijon's disco edits, slower edits of recognizable pop. This is *not* a Listening lane and selection should not be precious.
- **Price band.** Runners free / €0–15 token. Brand fee €1,800–3,500 per activation (per doc 03, §1.1).
- **Venue archetype.** A coffee roaster's terrace, a chiringuito post-race, a Patacona beach bar at noon, a Marina warehouse with garage doors open in summer.
- **Internal owner.** Alberto (with brother as alternate), with Madefor as creative partner.
- **Sponsor lane.** Sportswear (Hoka, On, Asics, NB, Saucony), nutrition (Maurten, Made for Drinks), recovery (Therabody, Whoop, Beam), coffee (Right Side, Toma, Three Marks).
- **Refusal.** No alcohol-led visuals, no club-late visuals, no peak-time selection, no Devialet rig (it's the wrong context — a Funktion-One or a JBL pole rig is correct).

### 5.2 Listening — the curated / Devialet-coded daytime lane

- **Positioning.** The dusk listening circle, Vespertina-format from doc 08. The Devialet-tier curated experience — the lane that Alberto explicitly wants Lowtide to be known for. *This is the lane that carries the Devialet partnership, the editorial press, the curatorial credibility.*
- **Audience.** 28–45, creative-class, design / hospitality / architecture / tech-creative. The Sync wellness-curious audience (per doc 07) plus the international Devialet/Aesop/Loewe-buyer profile.
- **Music posture.** Ambient, fourth-world, organic house, downtempo, modern composition. 60–95 BPM. Album-in-full nights. Hiroshi Yoshimura, Caterina Barbieri, Suzanne Ciani, Floating Points (in his Crush mode), Pional in his ambient register, Marc Piñol's slower work.
- **Price band.** €60–90 GA / €120–150 patron (per doc 07's revised ceiling).
- **Venue archetype.** Bombas Gens, Real Colegio del Patriarca cloister, Caro Hotel courtyard, a private modernist villa in El Saler, Lonja de la Seda for a flagship Vespertina.
- **Internal owner.** Brother (selection-led, per the wellness-coded posture), with Alberto on production.
- **Sponsor lane.** Devialet, KEF, Genelec for sound; Aesop, Le Labo, Loewe Casa, Hermès Maison for lifestyle; Apartamento, Cereal, Wallpaper, Neo2 for editorial press.
- **Refusal.** No mainstream selection, no run-club imagery, no glassware-in-foreground photography, no late-night visuals.

### 5.3 Privé — the co-owned underground / Mlab night

- **Positioning.** The Mlab-aligned underground night. Selection-led, club-credible, alcohol-friendly because the venue serves and the audience expects it. *Not a Lowtide-master post; co-owned with Mlab under a brand wall.* Alberto and brother appear on lineup under their **artist aliases** (per §3), not as "Lowtide."
- **Audience.** Valencia locals 22–32, scene-literate, low spend per head, high loyalty. The Mlab existing crowd plus a 20–30% expat/wellness-curious tail that crosses over from the other lanes.
- **Music posture.** House / techno / electro / trance / leftfield — whatever Mlab's scene is currently leaning toward. This lane is where Alberto and his brother get to push the boundaries of their selection without sponsor friction.
- **Price band.** €12–18 advance / €15–25 door. Door + bar split with venue, sponsor cash from a beer or a club-credible brand (Estrella Galicia, Mahou, Brugal — never Devialet).
- **Venue archetype.** Mlab's existing venue rotation. La 3, Spook, a Cabanyal warehouse, Killing Time, Mini Club. 2.4-licensed clubs, full DJ + dance + bar.
- **Internal owner.** Mlab counterparty (per doc 06, §7). Alberto/brother are bookings, not owners.
- **Sponsor lane.** Beer, club-credible spirits (Brugal, Beefeater Special Editions), local Valencia cultural press (CulturPlaza, Valencia Plaza nightlife). *Never overlapping with Listening's sponsor lane.*
- **Refusal.** No cross-tagging from @lowtide grid. No Devialet equipment. No wellness imagery. No 21-day window before/7-day window after a Listening or Flagship event (per doc 06, §6).

### 5.4 Flagship — the Lowtide Ritual annual / biannual moment

- **Positioning.** The 2× per year ticketed flagship that the Brief locks in. The Maratón Valencia weekend edition (December) and one summer edition (June or September). These are the case-study generators, the editorial moments, the Devialet-content engines. Production budget runs 2–3× a Listening event.
- **Audience.** A curated cross-section of all three other lanes + an international tail (wellness traveler, design-press, tier-1 Devialet/Loewe-tier guests). Capacity 200–400 depending on venue.
- **Music posture.** A *programmed arc* across the day (the Lowtide Ritual arc from the Brief's §90-Day Action 1). Sunrise breath → run → coffee → noon swim → afternoon Listening → sunset DJ. Multiple lanes' postures stitched into one day-long ritual. This is where Lowtide's "ritual-as-product" lives.
- **Price band.** €60–95 day pass, €150 patron tier with a printed program / vinyl mailer.
- **Venue archetype.** Albufera + Bombas Gens combined, or a private finca in El Saler, or a Cabanyal full-day takeover.
- **Internal owner.** Alberto + brother together (this is the only lane both run as co-leads). External producer for ops.
- **Sponsor lane.** Devialet (anchor) + Hoka or On (running anchor) + Estrella Galicia (regional anchor) + a hotel partner (Caro / Hospes / Only YOU). Stacked, not exclusive.
- **Refusal.** No standalone DJ booking — Flagship is *always* a programmed arc, never a one-set night. No undisclosed venue (sponsors need to plan). No surprise lineups (this is the lane that *requires* announced talent for press to land).

## 6. Essence pillars — values, not marketing copy

Four pillars, one per axis Alberto named. Each gets one declarative sentence, one "what we measure," and one "what we refuse." This is what the dashboard renders as the brand's spine, above the P&L.

### 6.1 We're improving as DJs.
The work is the work. Selection, mixing, beatmatching, transitions, reading rooms. Lowtide is at least partly a DJ practice; we measure ourselves against ourselves over time, not against a P&L.

- **What we measure.** Hours mixing per week per brother. Number of new tracks added to a working selection per month. One peer review per quarter from a DJ we respect (recorded mix sent for honest feedback). A yearly self-recorded set we can compare to last year's at the same date.
- **What we refuse.** Booking ourselves into rooms where we can't grow — sets where we have to play 100% mainstream because the brand demands it (those become mailbox-money curatorial work, not a Lowtide DJ booking). Long sets without preparation. Any gig where we'd be embarrassed to send the recording to a peer.

### 6.2 Community is the asset, not an output.
Real people, real relationships. The list (per doc 07) is named, knowable, and tended. We answer DMs. We remember names at the door. We grow slow on purpose.

- **What we measure.** Number of attendees who came to ≥3 events in the last 12 months (the "core"). Number of WhatsApp Broadcast list members who responded to the last drop. Number of names we know personally on the door at a 100-cap event (target: 30+).
- **What we refuse.** Audience-as-traffic vocabulary ("growth," "funnel," "conversion") in any *internal* document about community. Buying followers. Discount codes (per doc 07). Treating people as data.

### 6.3 Curation at Devialet-tier or not at all.
When we say "curated," we mean *designed* — the room, the rig, the order of moments, the light, the scent, the printed program. The selection is one input among many. We over-invest at flagship and Listening; we hold the line.

- **What we measure.** Acoustic measurement of the Listening room before each event. Single named photographer per event, briefed in writing. One printed artifact per event (program, zine, vinyl mailer). One tier-1 editorial press placement per Flagship.
- **What we refuse.** Vinyl banners. Generic LED walls in any Listening or Flagship setting. iPhone-only photography on flagship products. "We'll figure it out on the day" production. A Listening event without a printed program.

### 6.4 We don't trade essence for money.
Below a price floor, we walk. Above a brand-fit ceiling, we walk. The B2B-65/35 economic model from doc 03 is the *output* of this rule, not the rule itself.

- **What we measure.** Number of gigs declined this quarter for fit reasons (target: ≥1, because zero refusals means we're not curating ourselves). Brand-fit memo per B2B booking signed (one paragraph in `PROJECT.md`: why this brand, what we won't do for them, what we're asking back). Per-event "essence score" we self-rate 1–5 (was the room right? was the selection true? did the audience get what we promised?).
- **What we refuse.** Any gig under €1,500 (per Brief). Any sponsor conversation that requires us to play a specific track or genre. Any event that would force a club-coded photo onto the @lowtide grid. Any "exclusive" agreement that locks us out of Listening or Flagship work.

## 8. Implications for the dashboard

The dashboard reads as too monetization-focused because its current rendering is built around revenue per event and project status. The fix is structural — change the spine, not the styling.

**Five concrete changes, in priority order.**

1. **Replace the project-status spine with the 2×4 matrix.** The home view's primary frame is no longer "P&L by event." It's the 4 lanes (Afterglow, Listening, Privé, Flagship) × 4 essence pillars (improving as DJs, community, Devialet-tier curation, don't trade essence for money) matrix from §5.5. Each cell shows the most recent action and its non-monetary KPI alongside any revenue. **This single change reframes the dashboard from a revenue tool to a values tool.**

2. **Add a "Refusals" panel.** A visible count of gigs declined this quarter, with one-line reasons. Per the §6.4 essence pillar, zero refusals is a flag, not a feature. The dashboard surfacing refusals makes the brand discipline visible to Alberto and his brother every time they open it.

3. **Add a "DJ practice" panel.** Hours mixing per week per brother, new tracks added, peer reviews recorded, yearly self-comparison set. This is the §6.1 pillar made visible. **It probably belongs above the P&L.**

4. **Demote per-event revenue rendering to a secondary tab.** Per-event P&L is operationally critical but it should not be the front page. Move it to a "Bookings" or "Operations" tab. The home tab carries the matrix + the refusals + the DJ practice.

5. **Frame community as named relationships, not a number.** Replace "follower count" or "list size" rendering with three counts: the *core* (people who came to ≥3 events in 12 months), the *list-active* (responded to last drop), the *known-by-name* (door recognition). This is the §6.2 pillar made operational.

**Three reframings, lighter touch.**

6. **The dashboard's hero copy.** Instead of "B2B target / ticketed revenue / pipeline," the hero line should read something close to: *"Run, listen, breathe. The work is the work — and the work is paying."* Then the matrix below carries the operational reality. Tone-wise, the dashboard should sound like Alberto would describe the project to a friend on a walk, not to a CFO.

7. **The Lowtide Ritual arc on the home page.** Per the Brief's §90-Day Action 1, the Lowtide Ritual arc deserves a fixed slot on the dashboard's home view — minute-by-minute, the same way Daybreaker's home page is the format. This is the *what it actually is* anchor that prevents scope creep and reminds every booking decision what the product is.

8. **The four lanes get four small "lane cards"** on the dashboard's secondary level — each card showing the lane's positioning, the lane's owner, its sponsor lane, and its last event. These cards are how a future booking inquiry gets routed: a Hoka email goes to Afterglow, a Devialet email goes to Listening, a Mlab message goes to Privé, a Maratón Valencia opportunity goes to Flagship. **The dashboard becomes a routing system, not a status report.**
